Punjab National Bank was founded during the British rule in India when Punjab experienced rapid social and economic change after its annexation in 1849.
During this period, a new educated Indian class emerged with a strong desire for economic independence and freedom from colonial control.
One of the major aspirations of this new class was to establish a Swadeshi bank managed and financed by Indians.

The idea of creating a national Indian bank was first proposed by Rai Mool Raj of the Arya Samaj.
Rai Mool Raj believed that Indians should establish their own bank instead of allowing Indian capital to finance British banks and companies.
He argued that Indian money was being used to support foreign institutions while the profits were going entirely to the British.

Lala Lajpat Rai strongly supported the idea of establishing an Indian bank.
At the request of Rai Mool Raj, Lala Lajpat Rai circulated letters among influential Indian leaders advocating the establishment of an Indian joint stock bank.
This initiative became an important step in the constructive Swadeshi movement.

Lala Harkishan Lal, who had recently returned from England with knowledge of commerce and industry, also supported the idea and worked to implement it practically.

Punjab National Bank was officially founded on 19 May 1894.
The founding board of Punjab National Bank included prominent individuals from different regions, religions, and professional backgrounds.
The common objective of the founders was to establish a truly national bank that would serve the economic interests of India.

Punjab National Bank started its banking operations on 12 April 1895 in Lahore.
The first board of directors consisted of distinguished personalities from different communities.

The founding directors of Punjab National Bank included Sardar Dayal Singh Majithia, who was also the founder of Dayal Singh College and the Tribune newspaper.
Lala Lal Chand, one of the founders of DAV College, also served on the board.
Kali Prosanna Roy, a prominent Bengali lawyer and public figure, was another founding director.

The board also included Lala Harkishan Lal, who later became known as the first major industrialist of Punjab.
Other founding members included E.C. Jessawala, a well-known Parsi merchant of Lahore.
Lala Prabhu Dayal of Multan, Bakshi Jaishi Ram of Lahore, and Lala Dholan Dass of Amritsar were also among the founding directors.

These individuals represented different regions and communities including Bengalis, Parsis, Sikhs, and Hindus.
Their collaboration demonstrated a strong national spirit and commitment to building an Indian banking institution.

Sardar Dayal Singh Majithia served as the first Chairman of Punjab National Bank.
Lala Harkishan Lal served as the first Secretary of the Board.
Shri Bulaki Ram Shastri, a barrister from Lahore, was appointed as the first Manager of the bank.

Punjab National Bank declared its first dividend of four percent within seven months of starting operations.
Lala Lajpat Rai was the first customer to open an account with the bank.

The bank initially operated from a building located opposite the Arya Samaj Mandir in Anarkali, Lahore.
At the time of its establishment, the authorized capital of Punjab National Bank was two lakh rupees.
The initial working capital of the bank was twenty thousand rupees.

The bank began with a staff strength of only nine employees.
The total monthly salary expenditure for all employees was approximately three hundred and twenty rupees.

Punjab National Bank opened its first branch outside Lahore in Rawalpindi in the year 1900.
The bank experienced steady but gradual growth during the first decade of its existence.

In 1913, the Indian banking sector experienced a severe crisis after the failure of the Peoples Bank of India founded by Lala Harkishan Lal.
During this crisis, nearly seventy-eight banks failed across the country.
Punjab National Bank survived this financial crisis due to strong management and public confidence.

J.H. Maynard, who was the Financial Commissioner of Punjab at that time, acknowledged the bank’s resilience and praised its management.

During the global economic crisis following the 1929 Wall Street Crash, the banking industry worldwide faced severe turbulence.
Despite the difficult economic conditions between 1926 and 1936, Punjab National Bank continued to operate and maintain stability.

During this period, the Jallianwala Bagh Committee opened its account with Punjab National Bank.
This account was later operated by national leaders including Mahatma Gandhi and Pandit Jawaharlal Nehru.

Between 1941 and 1946, Punjab National Bank experienced a period of rapid expansion.
During these five years, the number of bank branches increased from seventy-one to two hundred and seventy-eight.
Bank deposits also grew significantly from ten crore rupees to sixty-two crore rupees.

Following the partition of India in 1947, Punjab National Bank faced major operational challenges.
On 31 March 1947, the bank decided to move its registered office from Lahore to Delhi.
Permission for this relocation was granted by the Lahore High Court on 20 June 1947.

After relocation, Punjab National Bank operated from Sreeniwas in Civil Lines, Delhi.
During the partition riots, several bank employees lost their lives while performing their duties.

The bank was forced to close ninety-two branches located in West Pakistan.
These branches represented approximately thirty-three percent of the total number of branches and held around forty percent of the bank’s deposits.

Punjab National Bank undertook major efforts to rehabilitate displaced account holders who had migrated from Pakistan.
The bank repaid depositors based on whatever documentation or evidence they could provide.
These actions strengthened public trust in Punjab National Bank.

After the partition crisis, the bank adopted a policy of rapid expansion to address surplus staff and rebuild its operations.
This expansion strategy helped Punjab National Bank achieve significant growth in the following decades.

In 1951, Punjab National Bank acquired the assets and liabilities of Bharat Bank Limited.
This acquisition made Punjab National Bank the second largest bank in the Indian private sector at that time.

In 1962, Punjab National Bank merged Indo-Commercial Bank with itself.
By July 1969, the bank’s deposits had increased from forty-three crore rupees in 1949 to more than three hundred and fifty-five crore rupees.
During the same period, the number of branches increased to five hundred and sixty-nine.

The bank’s advances also grew significantly from nineteen crore rupees in 1949 to two hundred and forty-three crore rupees by July 1969.
In 1969, Punjab National Bank was nationalized by the Government of India along with several other major banks.

Since its establishment in 1895, Punjab National Bank has served millions of customers across India.
The bank has also served several prominent national leaders including Jawaharlal Nehru, Govind Ballabh Pant, Lal Bahadur Shastri, Rafi Ahmed Kidwai, and Indira Gandhi.

Punjab National Bank has long been regarded as a people’s bank committed to supporting national development and economic growth.
